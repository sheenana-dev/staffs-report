# Team Health and Morale

## Purpose
Keep your teams engaged, motivated, and retained. In a 5-8 person department, losing even ONE person disrupts everything. Prevention is easier than recovery. This workflow helps you monitor morale and act before people disengage or leave.

## When
- Informally: Every day (observe energy in Staff GC and Management GC)
- Monthly: Quick pulse check with each department lead during 1-on-1
- Quarterly: Anonymous team health survey
- As needed: When you notice warning signs

## Steps

### Daily: Read the Room (2 minutes)
Scan the Staff GC for energy signals:
- Are people engaging with each other or just posting task updates?
- Are responses quick or delayed?
- Is anyone suddenly quiet who's normally active?
- Is the tone positive, neutral, or tense?

You don't need to act on every observation — just notice patterns.

### Monthly: Pulse Check with Leads (In 1-on-1)
Ask each department lead:
1. "How's the team's energy and morale right now?" (1-10 scale)
2. "Is anyone struggling or disengaged?"
3. "Is anyone at risk of leaving?"
4. "What would make the team happier?"

Take notes and compare month over month.

### Quarterly: Anonymous Team Health Survey
Send a simple anonymous survey (use Google Forms — free and anonymous):

**Questions:**
1. I feel valued for my contributions at B-Ticket (1-5)
2. I have what I need to do my job well (1-5)
3. I'm satisfied with the communication from leadership (1-5)
4. I see a future for myself at B-Ticket (1-5)
5. My workload is manageable (1-5)
6. I feel comfortable raising concerns with my lead (1-5)
7. What's one thing we should START doing?
8. What's one thing we should STOP doing?
9. What's one thing we should KEEP doing?

**Processing results:**
- Average score below 3.0 on any question = needs immediate attention
- Read open-ended responses for themes (not individual identification)
- Share summary results with the team (transparency builds trust)
- Pick 1-2 actions based on feedback and follow through

## Warning Signs Checklist

Watch for these across all departments:

**Individual Warning Signs:**
- [ ] Sudden decrease in communication
- [ ] Quality of work dropping
- [ ] Arriving late / leaving early consistently
- [ ] Not participating in team activities
- [ ] Becoming cynical or negative in group chats
- [ ] Asking about policies (leave balance, notice period) more than usual
- [ ] LinkedIn profile suddenly updated (classic pre-departure sign)

**Team Warning Signs:**
- [ ] Increased conflict between team members
- [ ] People doing the minimum instead of going above and beyond
- [ ] No one volunteers for new projects
- [ ] Side conversations replacing open team discussions
- [ ] High absence/sick leave usage
- [ ] "Us vs them" language about other departments or management

## Action Plans

### When Morale is Low (Score 2-3/5)
1. Talk to the department lead first — what do they think is causing it?
2. Have informal conversations with 2-3 team members (not interrogation — genuine check-in)
3. Identify the top 1-2 causes
4. Take ONE visible action within the week
5. Follow up in 2 weeks — did it help?

**Common causes and quick actions:**

| Cause | Quick Action |
|-------|-------------|
| Overwork | Review priorities, cut low-value tasks, hire if needed |
| Feeling unrecognized | Public recognition in All Hands group, personal thank-you |
| Unclear direction | Share the quarterly plan, explain why their work matters |
| Poor tools/equipment | Prioritize budget for upgrades |
| Conflict with a teammate | Mediate directly, don't let it fester |
| No growth opportunities | Discuss career paths, offer skill development |
| Bad communication from leadership | Be more transparent, share context for decisions |

### When Someone Wants to Leave
1. Don't panic. Have a private conversation.
2. Ask: "What's driving this decision?"
3. Listen fully before responding.
4. If it's fixable (role, pay, growth) — discuss what could change. Don't make promises you can't keep.
5. If it's not fixable (moving, career change, better offer) — respect their decision.
6. Begin transition planning immediately.
7. Conduct an exit interview (see template).

### When Conflict Arises
1. Address it within 48 hours — it only gets worse with time
2. Talk to each person individually first
3. If needed, bring them together with clear ground rules:
   - No interrupting
   - Focus on the issue, not the person
   - Goal is resolution, not winning
4. Document the agreed resolution
5. Follow up in 1 week

Link: [Google Form link]

I'll share the results and what we're doing about them next week.

### Exit Interview Questions
```
Exit Interview — [Name] — [Date]

1. What's your primary reason for leaving?
2. What did you enjoy most about working here?
3. What did you enjoy least?
4. How was your relationship with your direct manager?
5. Did you feel you had opportunities to grow?
6. Were there any issues that could have been addressed to keep you?
7. Would you recommend B-Ticket as an employer to a friend?
8. Any final advice for us?

(Record answers. Look for patterns across multiple exits.)
```

## Red Flags
- Survey response rate below 50% — people don't believe it's anonymous or don't care
- Same feedback appears survey after survey — you're collecting data but not acting on it
- Department lead says morale is fine but survey scores are low — lead is out of touch
- Multiple people leave the same department within 3 months — systemic issue in that team
- Team never socializes or jokes around — they're just clocking in and out
- You avoid the survey because you're afraid of the results — that's exactly when you need it most
