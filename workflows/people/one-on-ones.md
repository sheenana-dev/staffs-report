# One-on-One Meetings

## Purpose
Build trust with your department leads, understand what's really going on, and help them grow. 1-on-1s are the single most important tool for people management. This is where you hear things that never come up in group settings.

## When
- Weekly or bi-weekly with each department lead (4 leads = 4 meetings)
- 30 minutes each
- Same day/time every week — consistency builds trust
- Never cancel without rescheduling — it sends the message that they're not important

## Suggested Schedule

| Day | Time | Lead |
|-----|------|------|
| Tuesday | 1:00 PM | IT Lead |
| Tuesday | 2:00 PM | Design Lead |
| Tuesday | 3:00 PM | Sales Lead |
| Tuesday | 3:30 PM | Marketing Lead |

Keep them on the same day so you can compare themes and spot patterns.

## Steps

### Before the Meeting (2 minutes)
1. Review your notes from last time — any follow-ups?
2. Check their department's recent standup updates
3. Have ONE question prepared beyond the standard ones

### During the Meeting (30 minutes)

**Opening (5 min): Check in as a person first**
- "How are you doing?" (genuinely — not as a formality)
- "How's the team's energy level?"

**Their agenda (15 min): Let them lead**
- "What's on your mind this week?"
- "What do you want to talk about?"
- Let them bring up what matters most to them
- Listen more than you talk (aim for 70/30 — they talk 70%)

**Your items (5 min): Share what you need to**
- Updates that affect their department
- Feedback (positive or constructive)
- Questions about things you noticed

**Close (5 min): Action items and support**
- "What's the one thing I can do to help you this week?"
- Note any commitments you made
- Confirm the next meeting

### After the Meeting (5 minutes)
1. Write brief notes (3-5 bullet points) — what was discussed, action items, mood
2. Follow through on anything you committed to
3. Watch for patterns over time (recurring concerns, energy shifts)

## Templates

### 1-on-1 Notes Format
```
1-on-1 with [Name] — [Date]

Mood/energy: [good / okay / low / stressed]

What they brought up:
- [topic 1]
- [topic 2]

What I shared:
- [topic]

Action items:
- Me: [what I committed to]
- Them: [what they committed to]

Follow-up from last time:
- [item] — [done / in progress / not done]

Notes for next time:
- [anything to revisit]
```

### Telegram: Rescheduling a 1-on-1
```
Hey [name], I need to move our 1-on-1 this week. Can we do [alternative time] instead?

I don't want to skip it — your time matters to me.
```

## Question Bank

Use these when the conversation needs a spark. Don't ask all of them — pick 1-2 per meeting.

**About their work:**
- What's the most frustrating part of your job right now?
- What's going better than expected?
- If you could change one thing about how we work, what would it be?
- What's something you're proud of from this week?

**About their team:**
- How's morale on the team?
- Is anyone struggling that I should know about?
- Is the workload distributed fairly?
- Who on your team is ready for more responsibility?

**About you (their manager):**
- Am I giving you enough context on decisions?
- Is there anything I'm doing that makes your job harder?
- Do you feel comfortable pushing back on me when you disagree?
- What's one thing I could do better as a manager?

**About their growth:**
- What skills do you want to develop this quarter?
- Where do you see yourself in a year?
- Is there a project you'd like to lead?
- What part of your job energizes you the most?

**About blockers:**
- What's slowing your team down the most?
- Is there a process that's broken?
- Are you waiting on any decisions from me?
- What would make next week better than this week?

## Red Flags
- Lead says "everything is fine" every week — they don't trust the space yet. Keep being consistent.
- You do most of the talking — flip the ratio. Ask questions, then be quiet
- Action items from you are never completed — you're losing credibility. Follow through or don't commit
- Lead stops bringing topics — the meetings feel pointless to them. Ask: "How can we make these more useful?"
- Surprises in group settings that should have come up in 1-on-1 — trust isn't there yet
- You only talk about work tasks — 1-on-1s should also cover growth, feelings, and support

## Tips for New Managers
- It's okay to say "I don't know" — it's better than making something up
- Don't use 1-on-1 time for status updates — that's what standups are for
- Take notes during or immediately after — you will forget
- Start every 1-on-1 with their agenda, not yours — it shows you value their perspective
- If someone cries or gets emotional, don't panic — stay present, listen, acknowledge
- It takes 4-6 weeks of consistent 1-on-1s before people truly open up
